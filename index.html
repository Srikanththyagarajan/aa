Introduction

Nearly 1 in 5 people in the world have some kind of disability. That's over a billion people, or about 15% of the world's population. These disabilities come in many forms, from physical impairments such as vision, hearing or physical motor, to more hidden disabilities like autism, dyslexia, or other learning challenges. It is vital to consider these disabilities when building websites in order to ensure that everyone can use everything we build.

The AA+ Standard was created to help facilitate consistency with our brand guidelines. It incorporates concepts from the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.0 Standard, published by the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C), Complying with the AA+ Standard does not necessarily result in compliance with WCAG AA, BS 8878, ISO/IEC 40500 or any other documented standard

Guiding Principles

we work hard to design the best user experiences. This means all users, irrespective of age, impairment or disability, should feel delighted and satisfied with our work.

  • Accessibility should be addressed at inception. It's everyone's responsibility to learn how to identify barriers and remove them.
  • Always use semantic code. This is the best way to inform software of the meaning of our content as well as the content itself, and the downstream benefits are substantial.
  • Accessibility should support innovation, user experience, and business requirements, not compromise any of these. Embracing standards and building code in an accessible way is a method of delivering projects, not a way of defining them.

Accessibility does not and should not hinder design choices. There are always ways to make interfaces accessible. If you believe it is not technically possible to make some interface accessible, please talk to the Accessibility Software Engineering team early in the process, so there is enough time to include support in the frameworks and assistive technology tools.

Disabilities and Impairments

The following is a list of the major categories of impairments, although it is important to note that often users may have a combination of these.

It is also important to note that when we make improvements for accessibility, we make improvements for usability for everyone, not just users that fall within these categories.

Visual

Visual impairment includes those with no vision, or low vision. In 2010 it was estimated that 285 million people worldwide were visually impaired, of whom 39 million were blind.

Users with no vision will use screen reader software to access their technology. While individuals who are blind are unlikely to use mice with a computer, they do rely on extensive keyboard commands and refreshable braille displays to access their technology.

Low vision such as short- or long-sightedness may result in users using assistive technologies such as browser or screen zoom magnifiers. They may prefer text-only zoom in their browser, which zooms only the text and does not result in extra horizontal scrolling.

Colorblindness affects around 8% of all males. These users will not use any kind of assistive technology, so where color is vital (such as when choosing a colored product) this information must be visible to everyone.

Hearing

This category includes those who have no hearing at all ("deaf" or "profoundly deaf") or those with some hearing ("hard of hearing"). In 2013 it was estimated that 360 million people worldwide have disabling hearing loss.

It is important to consider audible information, and how to present it to those that may not be able to hear it. 1 in 1,000 people are born deaf, and some users are deafblind, meaning they have a combination of visual and hearing impairments. These users are likely to use a screen reader, combined with a braille display.

Motor

This includes those with varying types of physical disabilities that substantially limit movement and fine motor controls. These can consist of limited upper limb mobility, as well as manual dexterity and co-ordination challenges that impede the ability to access technology using mainstream methods. In many cases, this involves a profound long-term disability, but may also include individuals with a temporary motor impairment, such as a broken bone.

These users may struggle to use a mouse so keeping elements big and easy to interact with is important. Those that cannot use a mouse will use assistive technologies such as switches, joysticks, foot pedals or voice recognition software. These methods will often imitate the keyboard, so it is vital to support keyboard only input.

Cognitive

This includes those who have dyslexia (a condition which causes difficulty in reading, writing and/or spelling), autism, developmental delays or other learning difficulties. Cognitive disabilities range greatly in severity from mild to profound. Cognitive impairment is more common in the very young or old.

Because some cognitive delays may manifest in problems understanding information, these users (and in fact, all users) benefit from simple language, clear instructions and obvious calls to action or interaction indicators.

Speech

This includes those who are non-verbal or have speech impediments. There are few specific accessibility considerations for software for these users, other than with voice recognition, where it must be made certain that alternatives are available.

Where a function requires a telephone number to call, we need to consider the requirements of these users, and services such as Teletypewriters.

Environmental

Although not technically a physical impairment or disability, this includes those with some kind of environmental or situational issue that causes problems with the use of software. This includes slow connections, slow hardware, old software, and loud or dark environments.

To serve these users, do not automatically assume a fast connection and avoid big downloads – such as retina images – unless necessary.

Visual Design

Visual design involves the different skills and disciplines specifically involved in the visual presentation of software and websites. Design includes and affects the interactions and content that will make up an experience, and so it is vital to consider edge cases upfront, and understand the kind of difficulties that users may face.

  • Information Architecture

    Summary

    http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_architecture

    Information Architecture is the art of arranging information in consistent and intuitive ways, making it simple for users to absorb complex information. The purpose of Information Architecture is to analyze, organize and label information on websites so that users can find what they need. It should be well organised with good navigation, intuitive, logical, and make it easy to find information and complete tasks.

    Good Information Architecture will make it simple for users to locate specific information as well as discover new information, to complete tasks, and orient themselves within a process.

    It is useful to consider the audience and goals of the page (as well as the layout) early on in the process. It may help to consider the page as text-only initially, then add design and features.

    Rules

    • MUST: Content must be in a logical and intuitive order
      Web

      The reading order should match the visual order. To do this, the code must also match the reading order. It is helpful to test reading order with CSS disabled. This is particularly important for form fields and elements.

    • MUST: Information must be visually organised into manageable chunks
      Web

      It is helpful to plan the heading structure at the design stage, early in the process. Use blocks of color rather than vague outlines or shades when grouping visually to make it clear that this is a group to all users. This is especially important for mobile devices.

    • MUST: Users must be able to easily discover related content
      Web

      This can easily be achieved by providing a list of links of related content, if appropriate.

  • Typography

    Summary

    https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Typography

    Typography is the art and technique of arranging type in order to make language visible. From an accessibility perspective, it is important to keep text big enough for visually impaired users, and to avoid unusual fonts or formatting that may make it difficult for users to read. Appropriate spacing will also help users to distinguish the individual characters and understand grouping and hierarchy.

    Some fonts can be difficult to read for some users, particularly those with reading challenges, learning disabilities or attention deficit disorders. For example, research has shown that individuals with dyslexia tend to benefit from the use of serif fonts over sans serif. Some users prefer larger text. All users should have the ability to customize the size of fonts (by overriding stylesheets) and the layout should be fluid and able to accommodate.

    If using custom or non web-safe fonts, it is important to give thought to the font stack, and which fonts to fall back to if custom fonts are unavailable.

    Rules

    • MUST: Text that is not a link must not be underlined
      Web
    • MUST: Text must be appropriately sized
      Web

      A minimum font size of 12px is recommended to ensure text is easy to read for all users. Mobile Safari has a minimum font-size of 13px. This should not be overridden with -webkit-text-size-adjust. Many older users find it easiest to read font-sizes between 14px and 16px.

      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: The font-size must not be specified as less than 10px in CSS
        Web
      • SHOULD: Font-size should be defined in ems or rems, with a px fallback
        Web
      • SHOULD: Font-size for inputs should be a minimum of 16px
        Web If the font-size for inputs is below 16px, in iOS the browser will zoom the viewport automatically when fields are focused until their font-size is 16px.
    • SHOULD: Text should not be fully justified
      Web

      Left justification provides a framing reference for people with dyslexia (or right justification for right-to-left languages). The whitespace created by fully justified text creates patterns which makes it difficult to read and focus on the text.

    • MUST: Text must not be italicized
      Web

      This applies to large blocks of text.

    • MUST: Text must not be in all caps
      Web
      Implementation Guidelines
      • SHOULD: CSS should be used to control capitalization
        Web
    • MUST: Line lengths must not be of excessive length
      Web

      Avoid line lengths longer than 80 characters. These can be difficult to read when using a screen magnifier.

  • Zoom

    Summary

    Both OS X and iOS have built-in zoom functionality that allows users to magnify elements on the screen. Modern browsers, such as Safari, also allow the user to zoom just the text. Users may choose to enable this behavior, and may zoom in or out. It is necessary to ensure they have this ability to keep the website usable for them.

    Rules

    • MUST: Users must be able to use/comprehend the page with text zoomed
      Web

      Test form elements when zooming. An absent line-height or setting a fixed height rather than minimum-height will often cause issues here. The rules regarding zooming of text do not apply to closed captions or images of text.

      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: Users must be able to modify content size using browser settings and preferences
        Web
      • MUST: Site must be useable at 50% when 'Zoom Text Only' is used
        Web
      • MUST: Site must be useable at 200% when 'Zoom Text Only' is used
        Web
      • MUST: 'Zoom Text Only' must not cause extra horizontal scrolling
        Web
      • SHOULD: Text line-height should be defined in CSS to ensure text is readable when it spans over multiple lines, is zoomed or resized
        Web

        Consider testing zoom in longest language localisation to ensure text does not overlap. The value for line-height should not have a unit. It should be set somewhere between 1.5 and 2 (this is the equivalent of 150% and 200% respectively).

    • MUST: Users must be able to zoom the page appropriately on mobile devices
      Web
      Implementation Guidelines
      • SHOULD: The users' ability to scale should not be suppressed
        Web

        On mobile, ensure viewport metatag is set to user scalable and ensure maximum scale is at least 2.0.

  • Color

    Summary

    Color can be a great way of calling attention to items on a page, or to indicate state. However, it is estimated that 8% of males (and 0.5% of females) have a degree of color blindness and so it is vital not to rely on color to convey information and to augment it with alternative means.

    If color is vital (for example, for a colored product) visually label color in text on screen. Reference buttons and elements by their function and text, not their visual characteristics. Specifically placing red and green side-by-side can be hard to distinguish, so make sure to augment this so that colour is not the only distinction.

    If using color coding on maps and inforgraphics, also include another visual distinction that clearly communicates the meaning of the content without relying on color alone.

    For some users with visual impairment, a high level of contrast is necessary between text and the background for them to successfully distinguish and read it.

    To test color contrast, tools such as Color Oracle are helpful, or enabling grayscale in OS X. Photoshop even has built-in proofing tools to emulate types of color blindness.

    http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Color_blindness#Total_color_blindness

    Rules

    • MUST: Color alone must not be the only means of conveying information
      Web

      Good use of color can be used to enhance accessibility and usability i.e. color coding on navigation to indicate the user's position within the site. However, it is vital not to rely upon this behavior for color-blind users. Alternative means should be provided to convey information, indicate a reaction or prompt a response.

      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: Color that conveys information must have a perceivable alternative
        Web

        Examples of alternatives include text, shapes, images (with alternative text), icons (with alt attributes), patterns for graphs or pie charts. If using icons, use standard iconography that users already recognize and understand.

        For example, when showing a form error, do not rely on only a red outline or text color for a field to indicate the error, since many users may not be able to perceive this. Instead, augment this approach with a visible error or icon on screen.

    • SHOULD: Text and background colors should have sufficient contrast
      Web

      This applies to any content that conveys meaning such as images of text, links, buttons, controls and so on. It does not apply to text in logos.

      Consider using Lea Verou's color contrast checker.

      Implementation Guidelines
      • SHOULD: Contrast ratio should be at least 4.5:1 (luminosity)
        Web

        There are exceptions to this rule. Large text (18pt or 14pt bold) must have a contrast ratio of at least 3:1. Incidental text that is inactive, pure decoration, not visible, or part of a picture and logotypes are exempt.

        For mobile, it is recommended that text or an image of text has a contrast ratio of at least 7:1 (unless they are exempt, following the rules above).

      • MUST: All text colors and background colors must be specified in CSS
        Web
      • MUST: Fallback colors must be defined in CSS when using gradients
        Web

  • Visual Indicators

    Summary

    Visual indicators are vital to draw the user’s attention to an area of the page. They are also used to convey the users current focus position (for keyboard users), without which the user cannot orient themselves within the page. Where the browser and/or operating system has a default focus outline, it is best practice to utilize standard conventions, since the user relies upon them to understand their current position.

    Visual cues are also important to indicate to a user the kinds of interaction that are available for an element of the page.

    Rules

    • MUST: Visible focus must be present on interactive elements
      Web
    • MUST: The default focus outline must not be removed from interactive elements
      Web
    • MUST: It must be visibly obvious that an interactive element is interactive
      Web
    • MUST: The current state of an interactive element must be visibly obvious
      Web

      This includes selected, focused and disabled as well as combinations of these.

  • Animation

    Summary

    Moving or blinking elements on a page can be distracting to many users, particularly those with cognitive impairments.

    If items are moving on the page some users will find it difficult to read and interact. Some may need more time than others. Users with Aspergers syndrome, autism spectrum disorders or attention deficits may find it distracting. Some movement can help make areas of a page stand out, but this should be used with caution.

    Sometimes this moving content can be blocked by the user, so don't rely on it to convey important information that isn't elsewhere on the page.

    Rules

    • MUST: Content that blinks, scrolls or updates regularly must be used with caution
      Web
      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: Content must not flash more than 3 times per second
        Web
      • SHOULD: Automatic movement on the page should stop after 5 seconds or provide a mechanism to pause or stop the movement
        Web

        This includes animated GIFs, carousels, slideshows, tickers and so on.

      • SHOULD: Multiple parts of the same page should not move simultaneously
        Web

Content

Content refers to the text and images which populate the design and layout of websites. This is the essence of the website, and careful consideration must be given to how it will be exposed, the kinds of contexts in which it will be available, and how users (or machines) may consume it.

  • Titles

    Summary

    The page title appears in the browser chrome, at the top of the window. When using tabbed browsing, it appears within the tabs themselves. It also shows in bookmarks, or a history list.

    The title is the first piece of information presented to a user using a screen reader, and as such it needs to be descriptive and unique within the site.

    Rules

    • MUST: Page titles must be unique, clear and descriptive
      Web

      Titles must be succinct, meaningful, include keywords with which users search, and uniquely identify page and context.

      Implementation Guidelines
      • SHOULD: Every page's title should follow a consistent pattern across the site
        Web

        An example pattern might be to identify the page specifically at the beginning of the title, and to identify the site at the end, with a hyphen as a delimiter.

      • SHOULD: Repeated information should be placed at the end of the title
        Web

        Information should not be repeated in the title at all, if possible.

      • SHOULD: Page titles should not exceed 60 characters
        Web

        This is also important for SEO since titles will be visibly truncated at around this point. The visible text of the title is also likely to be truncated in tabs, bookmarks and so on.

      • MUST: Special characters in the title must be escaped
        Web

  • Headings

    Summary

    Headings are used by many assistive technologies to create a document outline. When a sighted user scans a website, they take note of headings, buttons and visual groupings. A document outline is a kind of mental map that a user with impaired vision will use to achieve the same thing. The headings in the page are the primary means of achieving this, so they must be clear and descriptive.

    Rules

    • MUST: Headings must clearly inform the user of the page structure
      Web
      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: Headings must be descriptive and accurate for the content of the page
        Web
      • MUST: Sibling heading text must be unique
        Web

        Headings of the same level (i.e. those that share the same parent heading) must be unique.

      • SHOULD: Distinguishing information should be placed at the beginning of a heading
        Web

        Screen reader users may not listen to the entire heading, so important information must be placed at the beginning.

  • Copy

    Summary

    All written editorial content that does not fit into a more granular category is defined as “copy.” Copy should be simple and clear, so that all users can understand it quickly and easily. For example, one should not assume that the primary languages of a country are the primary languages for inhabitants of that country – for those who are prelinguatorily deaf (those who are born deaf, or become deaf before they learn a language), sign language is likely to be their primary language. Keeping text clear but concise can benefit many different users.

    Rules

    • MUST: Pages must include content that is understandable when images are not perceivable
      Web
      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: The page must make sense when images are disabled
        Web
      • MUST (WITH EXCEPTIONS): Images of content must have alternative text that describes that content
        Web

        The alt attribute should contain valuable information about the image that is not included in the surrounding content.

        Decorative and redundant images are an exception to this rule, and must have empty alt attributes.

      • MUST: Text within images must be repeated in the alt attribute
        Web
      • MUST: Functional images must have alternative text that describes the function, not the content
        Web
      • SHOULD: Key information should be placed at the beginning of the alternative text
        Web
      • MUST: Alt attributes must not include terms that describe the element's role
        Web

        Do not include words like 'image', 'graphic', 'button, 'link' in alt attributes. It is acceptable to use terms like 'icon' or 'photograph' if the distinction is meaningful or important in that context.

      • MUST: Decorative images must have an empty alt attribute
        Web

        All decorative, unlinked foreground images must have an alt attribute set to an empty string (e.g. alt="").

        Images used as links must always have alternative text, or there will be no text associated with the link.

    • MUST: Clear instructions must be provided before asking the user to complete a task
      Web

      If the user is to be asked to perform a non-trivial task, explain ahead of time what the task will involve and what they will need.

      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: Instructions must be placed at the beginning of a form
        Web

        This does not apply to very small or simple forms, such as a login form.

    • MUST (WITH EXCEPTIONS): Instructions must not rely on shape, size, visual location, orientation or sound alone
      Web

      Ensure that instructions do not rely on only visual content, audio content or content that is perceived through a single sense alone.

      In some languages, it is commonly understood that "above" refers to the content previous to that point in the content and "below" refers to the content after that point. In such languages, if the content being referenced is in the appropriate place in the reading order and the references are unambiguous, statements such as "choose one of the links below" or "all of the above" would conform to this rule.

    • MUST: Operational Instructions must be device independent
      Web

      The user could be using any device to interact with the website. For this reason operational instructions must be device independent - e.g. Do not have links that say 'click here' or 'click the submit button'.

      It is recommended to use the word 'press', since this can be understood to apply to touch, mouse or keyboard.

    • MUST: Important information must be included at the beginning of content
      Web

      This includes links, labels for controls, headings, alt attributes, page titles and so on.

    • MUST: Terms identifying standard elements must not be included in their labelling text
      Web

      Labelling of links, buttons, form controls, tab panels, custom control etc. must not include the element name. Do not include "role info" like link, images, form controls, buttons, tabs, tables and so on in the computed text (e.g. alt attributes, labels, placeholders, legends, etc).

    • SHOULD: Non-literal text should be avoided
      Web
      Implementation Guidelines
      • SHOULD: Simple language should be used
        Web

        Colloquialisms and metaphors must not be used

        For example, "it's raining cats and dogs".

  • Localization

    Summary

    For an international website, it is important to remember to localize all content, not just the content that is visible.

    Rules

    • MUST: All content must be localized
      Web

      This relates to both visible text and other user-presentable text that is visually hidden, such as alt attributes and aria-label attributes.

      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: The language of the page must be defined
        Web

        The lang attribute of the page must be set on the <html> tag, with appropriate and valid ISO 639 language and country code combinations to define the language of the page.

        For example: <html lang="en-US"> where "en" means English (language), and "US" means the United States (country).

      • MUST: Changes in language within the page must be defined
        Web

        The "lang" attribute of elements in a different language to the page must be set on the element, with appropriate and valid ISO 639 language and country code combinations to define the language of the element. This can be used on any element.

        For example: <a href="page.html" lang="en-UK">link text</a> where "en" means English (language), and "UK" means the United Kingdom (country).

      • MUST: Reading direction must be defined for right-to-left languages
        Web

        The dir attribute must be used on the <html> element with a value of rtl for pages with right-to-left languages. Where there is a change in language in the page to a right-to-left language, this attribute must be set on the element surrounding this content.

      • MUST: Alternative text for images must be localized
        Web
      • MUST: <label> values must be localized
        Web
      • MUST: aria-label and aria-valuetext attribute values must be localised
        Web

Navigation

The ability to navigate is what sets apart websites from static documents. There are many methods by which a user can navigate a website, and it is essential to consider these, and how the user will interact with and understand them.

  • Links

    Summary

    By far the most commonly used mechanism to browse a website is via links. Therefore, links must be clear and obvious.

    Rules

    • MUST: Each link must make sense out of context
      Web

      Links must have enough context from the link text alone, and not depend on the surrounding content for meaning and purpose. Avoid phrases such as 'click here', 'more', 'continue' or 'learn more' as a user may access these without context and be unable to understand their purpose.

      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST (WITH EXCEPTIONS): Link text must not be repeated
        Web

        Multiple links on the same page that have the same computed text value must link to the same destination. Unless links go to the same destination, link text must be unique.

    • SHOULD: Links should not contain excessive text
      Web

      Use HTML5 block level links with caution. If a link is very long, a screen reader user may not listen to the entire link. Try to keep link text under 400 characters.

      Implementation Guidelines
      • SHOULD: Related links should be grouped
        Web

        Use lists to group related links. If multiple links are in close proximity that link to the same location, it is better to wrap this in a single link. Consider focus and hover styles for large block level links, do not rely on focus outlines alone if the link area is large.

      • SHOULD: Links should not use the title attribute
        Web

        Avoid the title attribute on links, it is not available to some assistive technologies. In addition, the visual tooltip behavior is not reliable, particularly to keyboard only users.

      • SHOULD: Event handlers should not be used to emulate links
        Web

        Do not add event handlers to elements to emulate links, use real links. Events may not be exposed correctly and impose cognitive load if users have to learn how to use them. If it is impossible to use a real link, use WAI-ARIA to expose it as a link or button.

    • SHOULD: Distinguishing information should be placed at the beginning of a link
      Web

  • Mechanisms

    Summary

    Users browse websites in different ways. Some use standard navigation to drill down, others search, and still others might use a sitemap or other means to find content. For this reason it is important to supply multiple mechanisms to browse a website and both locate and discover information and content.

    Rules

    • SHOULD: Navigation should be consistent
      Web
      Implementation Guidelines
      • SHOULD: Repeated components should be placed in the same location consistently across multiple pages
        Web

        Navigation mechanisms e.g. site navigation bars, site search and so on, that are repeated across pages should be consistent and be represented in the same order.

    • MUST: Methods must exist to allow the user to quickly access sections of the page
      Web

      Complex pages could use in-page links (a table of contents), or be split over multiple pages with pagination. Landmark roles (preferred) or skiplinks can be used for users of assistive technologies.

      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: Skip links must be visible when focused
        Web
    • MUST: Standard browser controls must work as expected
      Web
    • SHOULD: The user's position within the site should be clearly indicated
      Web

      Breadcrumbs will help users visualize where they are within the site, and make it easy to navigate back to previous web pages.

    • MUST: The user's position within a multipart flow must be clearly indicated
      Web

      Make it clear to a user if they are within a step of a multipart process or flow, so they understand how many steps remain. You could also indicate location within the site using visual cues such as breadcrumbs.

    • SHOULD: A site map should be provided
      Web
      Implementation Guidelines
      • SHOULD: The site map should provide an overview of the entire site
        Web

        The site map should help the user understand how the site is organized. This is a useful alternative to complex navigation.

      • MUST: The site map must be kept up-to-date
        Web
      • SHOULD: Every page should have a link to the site map
        Web
    • MUST (WITH EXCEPTIONS): Search functionality must be provided
      Web

      This does not apply to very small sites where it would be inappropriate. When implementing search functionality, the results page must include the search term the user entered in the page content, and in the title.

    • SHOULD: New windows should not be used
      Web
      Implementation Guidelines
      • SHOULD: New windows should not be opened without prior warning
        Web

        Give users advanced warning when opening a window. This can be done via hidden text stating "(opens in new window)", or with an icon with an appropriate alt attribute such as this one . Do not use the "title" attribute for this purpose, since it cannot be perceived by many assistive technologies.

      • SHOULD: New windows should not be opened without the users' consent
        Web

        Do not open new windows when a page is loading or when selecting radio buttons or checkboxes. The user must be aware that a new window will open, and consciously approve this behaviour.

    • MUST: iFrames must be used with caution
      Web

      Avoid the use of iFrames to display content to the user. If necessary, iFrames can be used with JavaScript to programmatically test features, but the iFrame must not be perceivable or navigable to all users (including assistive technologies).

      When an iFrame must be used by a third party vendor, the content must follow the AA+ standard. Dynamic content must be perceivable, and focus must be controlled appropriately.

      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: iFrames must have a "title" attribute that describes their content
        Web
    • SHOULD: There should be multiple ways to navigate
      Web

      Users choose to navigate in different ways, and different methods are intuitive or unintuitive to different users. For this reason, we must provide multiple methods of navigating, to better serve a wider range of users.

Images

Images bring to life a page that is otherwise just text and space. However, to many users, images are unavailable for various reasons, and so it is important to consider these users and what content to expose (or not expose) as alternatives.

  • Alternative Text

    Summary

    Users with visual impairments may not be able to see images, and others may disable images to save on bandwidth (for example, on a 3G network). For this reason, the image element supports an "alt" attribute, which is a piece of text designed to act as an alternative to the image.

    Without alternative text, an image is effectively invisible to these users, so give care and attention when writing this content.

    Rules

    • MUST (WITH EXCEPTIONS): All images must have an appropriate alt attribute
      Web

      Decorative images are an exception to this rule, and must have empty alt attributes. That is, alt="", not a missing alt attribute.

      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: Images of text placed inside a heading must have alt attributes
        Web

        Images with meaning that are children of headings must have an alt attribute value (or some other form of text alternative).

      • MUST: <input type="image" /> must have an appropriate, localized alt attribute
        Web
      • MUST: Images that convey information must not be set as background images or psuedo elements in CSS
        Web
      • MUST: If an image is the sole child of a link the alt attribute must describe the purpose of the link and its destination
        Web

  • Images of Text

    Summary

    Although it is always preferable to have "system text" (text that can be displayed in the browser without the author having to do anything special) there are some situations where it is necessary to use images of text e.g. for a special font or logo.

    Special attention must be given to the alt attributes for these images.

    Rules

    • MUST (WITH EXCEPTIONS): Images of text must not be used
      Web

      An exception to this rules exists where a particular font or style cannot be adequately represented with system text or a web-safe font.

      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: Text in images must be at least as large as the default system font
        Web

        Images of text do not scale well when using zoom functionality.

      • SHOULD: Images that contain decorative text should have an empty alt attribute
        Web

        Images that contain decorative text must have an empty alt attribute ( alt=""), for example, where text is legible on the screen of an image of a product.

  • Decorative Images

    Summary

    Many images serve no value except as decoration. For users of assistive technologies, these images just add unnecessary content and noise to the page. They should be made (effectively) invisible to these users.

    Rules

    • MUST: Images that do not convey content or meaning must be hidden from assistive technologies
      Web
      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: Decorative images must not be perceivable to assistive technology
        Web

        Images that are purely decorative and have no additional value or context to its surrounding content, should have an empty alt attribute or be set as a background image in CSS.

      • MUST: Empty alt attributes must not contain spaces or other characters
        Web The code should show alt="", not omit the alt attribute.

  • Infographics

    Summary

    http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infographic

    An infographic is an image displaying charts or methods for representing complicated data. Special considerations need to be given to how to expose this data to assistive technologies.

    Rules

    • MUST: Infographics must be perceivable to all users
      Web
      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: Images must not be used to display a data table
        Web
      • MUST: Alternative content must be provided if the infographic can not be made accessible in its current format
        Web
      • MUST: Infographics must have concise, accurate and descriptive alt attributes
        Web
      • SHOULD: Long alternative content should be exposed using a <figure> and <figcaption>
        Web
      • MUST: Infographics must be accessible to those with visual impairments such as low-vision or color-blindness
        Web

        Alternative content must not rely on color – e.g. pie charts should use both color and an accessible alternative. Consider color, font size, contrast, simple language, legends on graphs, use of abbreviations and so on.

      • MUST: Accessible alternatives must be provided for graphical symbols or icons that are displayed using Unicode font glyphs
        Web

        Use an image with an alt attribute value for graphical symbols and icons rather than a Unicode font glyph – e.g. an 'x' mark could indicate an item is out of stock but a user of assistive technology is only exposed to 'x' and does not know the intended meaning.

        Alternatively, provide an aria-label, for example: <button aria-label="delete">x</button>.

Audio/Video

Multimedia capabilities can include audio or video on a website. Whilst these formats of media can better convey various kinds of information and engage the user, many users are unable to perceive this information for various reasons. Careful care and attention must be given to alternatives for these users.

  • Alternative Formats

    Summary

    When considering audio and video, it is necessary to consider those who may be unable to see, hear, or both. For those users, an alternative or complementary means must exist to convey the missing information to the user.

    This should include subtitles, audio descriptions, and transcripts.

    Where the source is in a different language as to that of the target audience, subtitles must also be provided.

    Rules

    • MUST: Alternatives to audio and video content must be exposed to assistive technologies
      Web
      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: Synchronized captions must be provided
        Web

        If there are time constraints on provided alternative content, providing captions takes priority over providing transcripts. Transcripts may be generated from the captions.

      • SHOULD: Transcripts for video and audio content should be provided
        Web

        It may not be feasible to provide transcripts at the same time as the content. If this is the case, it must be provided as soon as reasonably possible.

      • SHOULD: Audio descriptions should be provided for video content
        Web
      • SHOULD: Alternatives for video and audio should be available in the content order immediately before the video and audio
        Web

        This could be a full alternative or a link to the alternative. Provide a link to the video or audio file so that the user can choose to open the file in their own preferred playback software.

      • SHOULD: Captions and transcripts should state if music or a soundtrack is used
        Web
      • SHOULD: Where a video has no audio, this should be made clear to the user
        Web

        Failure to do this could confuse a user as to why the clip is silent.

  • Multimedia Player

    Summary

    A multimedia player is the mechanism used to control and play back media such as audio and video. All modern browsers provide native mechanisms for this behavior, and these are preferred over complicated custom-built solutions.

    Rules

    • MUST: Audio and video must not autoplay on load of the page
      Web
    • SHOULD: Native browser and operating system controls should be used
      Web
    • MUST: Users must be able to control video and audio playback
      Web
      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: Controls to stop, pause, play and control volume must be independent of the user's system
        Web

        The user must be able to modify without adjusting the volume of their assistive technology.

Forms

Forms allow the user to input and select information and submit it to a website. These can be used for anything from performing a search, to completing a purchase. Forms can be intimidating to all users, but incomplete implementations can make them impossible to use for many users. It is important that forms are built and designed well, so that users can understand and use them.

  • Fields

    Summary

    A form field is any field that accepts data. These include text boxes, checkboxes and textareas. Since fields can have a number of states (for example, checked, selected or invalid) it is important to test that these states are being exposed properly to VoiceOver or other assistive technologies.

    Rules

    • MUST: Field types must be appropriate for the type of data they will be used to gather
      Web

      Use of any type of control that refines the amount of options available to the user will make it easier for the user to enter data with, and helps to avoid errors. Use checkboxes, radio buttons or dropdowns rather than free text input whenever appropriate.

      On mobile, there are even more well-supported input modes e.g. dates, text, numbers, language and more.

      Implementation Guidelines
      • SHOULD: Native form controls should be used whenever possible
        Web
      • MUST: Fallbacks must be provided when native form controls are not supported by the browser
        Web

        When using more modern input types like email, date and range, make sure to provide fallbacks in case these input types are not supported by the browser.

    • MUST: Form fields must have a sensible and logical tab order
      Web
    • MUST: Disabled fields must be identifiable
      Web

      If a native form element is disabled, use the disabled="disabled" attribute. If it is a custom ARIA control, use aria-disabled="true" instead.

    • MUST: Required fields must be identifiable
      Web

      Use required="required"` on native fields or aria-required="true" on custom fields.

  • Labels

    Summary

    A "label" is an HTML element that can be associated with a form field. The labels must always exist and should be visible at all times. Without a label, a user of assistive technologies will find it difficult to orient themselves, and to understand what data is expected in this field.

    Rules

    • MUST: All fields must be clearly labeled
      Web

      Every input that can contain or capture data must have a <label>.

      Implementation Guidelines
      • SHOULD: Labels for checkboxes or radio buttons should be placed immediately after the field
        Web
      • MUST: Labels must indicate required data formatting
        Web
      • MUST: Fields in multipart forms must each be labeled
        Web

        Multipart form fields (e.g. using three text boxes for a telephone number) must each have labels associated, although they do not all need to be visible.

      • MUST: Buttons must have unique labels
        Web

        Multiple buttons with different behavior on the same page must have different computed text, even if visually they have the same text. Buttons must have computed text that makes sense out of context. Override the value or contents with aria-label if necessary.

      • SHOULD: Form fields should have a visible <label>
        Web
      • MUST: Association of labels and their controls must be visually and programmatically determinable
        Web

        The <label> must be perceivable at assistive technology, even if visually hidden. The <label> must be explicitly associated with the input/control using the for attribute. Do not use the implicit method (containing the field within the <label>). Clicking a form element <label> should set focus to the associated form control.

      • MUST: <label>s must contain text
        Web

        If using images inside of <label> give them empty alt attributes as they are not always exposed to assistive technology. Instead, make sure alternative text is available within the <label>. The image could be placed in CSS instead.

        Using aria-labelledby will override the <label> element. Input controls can use aria-labelledby to reference one or more labelling elements via space separated unique IDs to provide more descriptive information.

      • MUST: Placeholder text must not be used without a <label>
        Web

        Placeholder text is not a substitute for a <label>. Placeholder text is an optional example of content for a field, whereas a <label> is required to label the field itself.

      • SHOULD: Custom placeholder implementations should not be used
        Web

        Standard placeholder implementations should be used; custom implementations may fail to hide or show the placeholder appropriately in all situations.

      • MUST: Buttons (inputs with type of button, submit or reset) must have descriptive value attributes
        Web

        In iOS use labels, hints and traits to provide alternatives for content and functionality. An iOS label should be short words or phrase. An iOS trait should describe type, and more than one can be used – e.g. button and selected. An iOS hint should be used sparingly, as an explanation, not as a command.

      • SHOULD: <label>s should not be generated by JavaScript
        Web

        Do not rely on JavaScript to generate <label>s.  These must be available without JavaScript, unless the field itself is also generated with JavaScript.

    • MUST: Associated controls must be grouped and labeled
      Web
      Implementation Guidelines
      • SHOULD: <fieldset> should be used to group related sets of controls
        Web

        A <fieldset> should be used to group related controls – e.g. groups of radio buttons (grouped by name), groups of checkboxes (grouped by name) or group of related fields e.g. "billing details".

      • MUST: A <fieldset> must contain more than one form control
        Web
      • MUST: A <fieldset> must not be the only child of a form
        Web
      • MUST: A <fieldset> must have a <legend>
        Web

        A <fieldset> must have exactly one <legend> and it must be the first child of the <fieldset>.

      • SHOULD: Related options within a `
        Web

  • Buttons

    Summary

    Buttons in forms include submit buttons (which will submit a form), reset buttons (which should not be used) and button elements themselves (which need to have custom behavior attached to them).

    Rules

    • MUST (WITH EXCEPTIONS): All forms must have a submit button
      Web

      There must always be a submit button for JavaScript disabled behavior. If it is made clear to the user that modifying a control will initiate a change in context, that condition is an exception to this rule. However, a submit button is always preferred to this technique.

      Implementation Guidelines
      • SHOULD: A submit mechanism should be provided to submit data
        Web

        Use <button type="submit">, <input type="submit"/> or <input type="image"/> to submit form data. Use <form> so that you can capture the submit event, and the user can submit the form with the return key or with the submit button.

      • SHOULD: Forms should not be submitted in reaction to an onChange event
        Web

        The user must make a conscious choice to change context. Provide a submit button to initiate this, rather than detecting a change in a field's value.

    • SHOULD: Reset buttons should not be used
      Web

      Avoid using <input type="reset"/> as these buttons are often activated in error, and rarely offer useful functionality to the user.

  • Error Handling

    Summary

    Populating and submitting a form can be a time-consuming and laborious process for individuals with special needs, and so it is important to help the user complete the form successfully the first time. Many users upon failing a task such as this will become disheartened, although a failure to convey to the user what is expected of them is a fault of the website author, and not the user.

    Where an error occurs with a form submission despite best efforts to help avoid it, the nature of the error and how to resolve it must be clear and simple. Specific examples of expected content should be provided where appropriate. It must be obvious which field or fields have issues, and simple for the user to fix the problem.

    Rules

    • MUST: Help must be given to users to avoid input errors
      Web
      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: Required fields must be indicated
        Web

        Use both aria-required="true" and required="required" attributes on form fields that are not optional, and keep the values in sync.

      • MUST: Users must be informed upfront of irreversible actions
        Web

        Use aria-describedby on form input elements for additional instructions.

      • MUST: Context sensitive help must be provided
        Web

        Context-sensitive help can include help links on every webpage, spell checking and auto-suggest, providing text instructions and examples of expected data formats.

      • MUST: A mechanism for error prevention must be provided
        Web

        To prevent errors either allow submissions to be reversible, or provide a mechanism for reviewing and correcting data before the final submission. This is of particular importance for personal and financial data.

    • MUST: Error messages must tell the user how to correct the error
      Web

      Error messages should not make the user feel at fault.

      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: Users must be informed when an error has occurred
        Web

        If an error occurs, do not do nothing. Inform the user, provide instructions to them on how to fix the error, and move keyboard focus to the first invalid field (if applicable, otherwise move focus to the error itself).

      • MUST: Error messages must be visibly and programatically associated with the corresponding fields
        Web

        All form errors should be visible in text and placed adjacent to the field with the error. If the error is placed at the top of the page, include a link to the field that has the error. There must be a one-to-one relationship with error and field. The errors and instructions for resolution must be perceivable to assistive technology users. Use the aria-describedby attribute to programmatically relate the error with the field.

      • SHOULD: Descriptions of errors should be provided in text
        Web

        Provide text descriptions of the errors, identify required fields not completed and identify values that are not allowed within a list of allowed values or formatting. Set aria-invalid="true" on form fields that contain invalid data – don't forget to remove the attribute once the data is valid.

      • SHOULD: Validation should be performed on the client-side before form submission
        Web
      • MUST: Color must not be the only indicator of errors
        Web

        If colour is used, be sure to augment it with text or icons.

    • MUST: Feedback must be provided when data is submitted successfully
      Web

  • Time Limits

    Summary

    Some users – in particular, those with motor impairments – can take a long time to achieve what others may be able to in a matter of seconds. For this reason, it is vital to avoid any kind of time limit wherever possible. If a time limit is necessary, the user should be able to extend this time limit.

    Rules

    • MUST (WITH EXCEPTIONS): Users must be able to complete an action or process without time limits
      Web

      Non-interactive synchronized media and real time events (such as auctions) are exempt from this rule.

      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: Users must be informed about the presence of a time limit
        Web
      • MUST (WITH EXCEPTIONS): Users must be able to control time limits
        Web

        If there is a time limit, the user must be able to either turn off the time limit, adjust the time limit to allow extension at least 10 times, or change the time limit to 10 times the original limit.

        Real-time events (like auctions) and time limits longer than 20 hours are exempt from this rule.

      • MUST: Users must be notified at least 20 seconds before a time limit runs out
        Web

        The notifications must be accessible alerts or dialogs with keyboard focus.

      • SHOULD: Users should be able to resume an activity after re-authentication
        Web

        When a session expires, the user must be able to continue their activity without losing any previous data after re-authenticating. Personally identifying information or financial data is exempt from this rule.

  • CAPTCHAs

    Summary

    http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CAPTCHA

    A CAPTCHA is a mechanism to determine whether the current user is a human being or a machine. It stands for "Completely Automated Public Turing test to tell Computers and Humans Apart". This normally involves showing the user a distorted picture of text (which a computer would find difficult to decipher), and asking the user to confirm the text as a means of identifying them as a human being.

    Although useful to prevent abuse of a website, mechanisms such as these serve to protect the website itself, and are not for the benefit of the user. Those with visual or cognitive disabilities (and many others) have a great deal of difficulty in reading the distorted text, or cannot see it at all. In this instance, it is necessary to provide an audio CAPTCHA, which is a distorted reading of a series of letters or numbers. These are equally difficult to decipher, and the deafblind are left with no mechanism at all.

    A number of services exist where users who cannot decipher the CAPTCHA can submit it to a human being, who will decipher it for them, and yet more services abuse the system to try to trick users into solving CAPTCHAs on their behalf.

    Since there is no reasonable way to make a CAPTCHA accessible, they should not be used.

    Rules

    • SHOULD: CAPTCHAs should not be used
      Web

      If it is necessary to provide a CAPTCHA, there must be access to a human customer service representative who can bypass this mechanism on the user's behalf, and this system must have Telecommunications Device for the Deaf capability.

Interaction

There are many ways in which a user can interact with a computer, and different methods better suit users with different needs. Users will have a predefined understanding and expectation of how software should work. Good interaction design is intuitive, obvious and predictable, with no cognitive load on the user.

  • Input Devices

    Summary

    Although most desktop users use a mouse or trackpad as their primary input device, many users with motor impairment will use (or emulate the use of) a keyboard as their primary device. It is therefore crucial that everything a mouse user can do, a keyboard user can do.

    The same applies to touchscreen users, and whatever other input devices might be coming such as game controllers, gestures and so on.

    It is vital to consider all input types, and not depend on any particular type of device when building and planning interactive elements.

    Rules

    • MUST: Interactions must be device independent
      Web

      Interaction must be device independent. Do not rely on the mouse, and consider touch and other input methods. In particular, all browser-based functionality must be operable using standard keyboard controls.

      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: Hover states must not be the only method of conveying information
        Web

        Information should also be visible on focus.

      • MUST: Mouse events must have equivalent keyboard events
        Web

        Use mousedown with keydown, mouse up with key up, click with keypress, mouseover with focus, and mouseout with blur.

      • MUST: If custom controls are required, make them focusable and respond to appropriate keyboard events
        Web
    • MUST: All browser-based functionality must be fully keyboard accessible
      Web

      All browser-based functionality e.g. using form controls, menus, drag and drop, selecting text, resizing, and regions must be available to keyboard users. Video and multimedia players must have controls that are fully keyboard accessible (using native controls is the best way to achieve this). Full keyboard access does not exist in iOS.

      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: Default keyboard controls must not be overridden
        Web
      • MUST: There must be at least one way of dismissing an overlay
        Web

        Pressing the escape key should dismiss overlays, modal dialogs, tooltips and so on. Alternative means to achieve this may include close buttons, cancel buttons or clicking outside of the element.

      • MUST: Links must be actionable by pressing the Return key
        Web
      • MUST: Buttons must be actionable by pressing the Return or Space keys
        Web

        If a link is performing an action like a button, expose that link as a button using the WAI-ARIA role="button" attribute and implement keyboard behavior using JavaScript.

    • MUST: Interactive elements must have sufficient target areas
      Web

      Maximize the clickable area of buttons and links so that they are easier to use. Make sure they are finger-read-tap friendly for mobile. In iOS 44px by 44px (CSS pixels, not screen pixels) should be considered a good minimum size for an interactive element. Target areas and their content should be consistent – e.g. links, paragraph and image or just image and link.

  • Managing Focus

    Summary

    When a keyboard user moves through a website, they move "focus" from one element to another by pressing the tab key or equivalent. When creating a new piece of content, or removing a piece of content that currently has focus, it is important to consider where the user's focus should move to.

    It is also important that the keyboard user can always move focus to the browser toolbars, otherwise they may find themselves unable to use the other functions of their browser.

    Rules

    • MUST: The user's focus must not be lost
      Web

      Screen readers jump to the top of the page or window if focus is lost or the currently focused DOM element is removed.

      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: Visible focus state must be indicated on custom focusable elements
        Web
      • MUST: Elements must be focusable before attempting to move focus to them
        Web

        Use tabindex if required, before moving focus to an unfocusable element - otherwise the browser may throw an error or there may be unexpected results.

      • MUST: Hidden elements must not be available to the user for focus
        Web

        Hidden elements ( display:none or visibility:hidden) will never trigger focus events or the :focus pseudo-class. Conversely, an element hidden with opacity:0 will be focusable. To be certain that a form field is not focusable use disabled="disabled" or display:none.

      • MUST: Focused elements must not be hidden
        Web

        Focusable elements should be displayed while they have focus. Only when focus is moved away may the elements be visually hidden.

      • MUST: Focus must not be removed when focus is received
        Web

        Never call the blur() method.

    • MUST: Users must be able to move focus away from a container or element
      Web
      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: Users must not encounter keyboard traps
        Web

        A keyboard trap is a situation where the user is unable to move the focus away from a container or element. The user must always be able to move focus out of a subset of content, and be able to access the browser toolbars.

    • MUST: Fields must not be automatically focused on page load
      Web

      Mobile Safari and VoiceOver ignore autofocus.

    • SHOULD: Items that are not actionable should not be keyboard focusable
      Web

  • Native Controls

    Summary

    Browsers make a number of native controls available by default, with accessibility built-in. These controls behave in exactly the same way as the rest of the user's operating system, and are consistent in look and feel to what the user is used to. Some examples might be buttons, checkboxes and dropdown list boxes.

    Using these native controls ensures the user's preferences are enforced, and any special behavior or considerations they need are already implemented.

    Rules

    • MUST: Native operating system and browser controls must be used wherever possible
      Web

      Custom controls are a big cost for engineering time, and difficult to make accessible. Use browser default controls for interactive elements and forms – keyboard behavior is then applied with no effort from the developer.

      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: Default interaction methods must not be overridden
        Web

        For example, if the user expects the up and down cursor keys to move through options in a dropdown listbox, do not disable or replace this behaviour.

      • SHOULD: Styling of form controls should not remove their meaning and be consistent
        Web
    • MUST: User interaction with standard operating system and browser controls must not be modified
      Web

      This includes the browser toolbars and behavior such as forward, back and jumping to bookmarks.

  • Custom Controls

    Summary

    Where there are no appropriate controls available in HTML 5, it is necessary to create them from scratch. This should be done as a last resort. When implementing custom controls, it is necessary to consider all types of interaction and expected behavior, and to expose this behavior correctly. Some examples include tab controllers, auto-suggestion, and image galleries.

    Rules

    • MUST: All interactive elements must be focusable
      Web

      Because most mobile platforms do not use a desktop-like keyboard model, tabindex does not work the same. Links must have an href attribute in order to be focusable (or use tabindex="0"). Use tabindex="0" to place a normally non-focusable element into the default tabbing order. Use tabindex="-1" to make a normally focuable element non-focusable, and to make a non-focusable element programmatically focusable using JavaScript.

      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: Positive tabindex value must not be used
        Web

        Avoid positive tabindex values, as use of these usually results in an illogical tab order.

      • MUST: Elements must be inserted into the DOM in the correct order to ensure for logical navigation and reading order
        Web
      • MUST: Dialogs and menus that are appended to the end of the DOM must use managed focus to ensure a logical navigation order
        Web
    • MUST: Custom widgets must have a sensible and logical tabbing order
      Web
    • MUST: Focus must be managed appropriately within dialogs
      Web

      This includes overlays, menus, and so on.

      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: Focus must be programatically set to the dialog when it is opened
        Web

        This includes overlays, menus and so on.

      • MUST: When open, keyboard focus must remain within the dialog
        Web

        This includes overlays, menus, and so on. Keyboard focus should remain within the contents of a modal dialog (overlay) and not move to the context underneath until it has been dismissed. However, the user must still be able to move focus to the browser toolbars to avoid a keyboard trap.

      • MUST: Focus must be returned to the triggering element when the dialog is closed
        Web

        This applies to overlays, menus, etc.

    • MUST: Access and shortcut keys must not be used
      Web

      They interfere with default browser controls and assistive technologies.

  • Changing Content

    Summary

    Often in websites sections of content update without reloading the page. For example, when applying a filter or resorting content, or a live chat window where new pieces of content are appearing. It can be difficult for users of assistive technology to detect or avoid these changes. For this reason, it is necessary to give the user full control of this behavior, and go to lengths to ensure it is exposed to them.

    Rules

    • SHOULD: Users should be able to control when and if a content change will occur
      Web
      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: Users must be notified if the page will be updated in reaction by form controls
        Web

        Describe to the user what will happen before the change occurs. Instructions should precede the control and preferably be programmatically associated with it.

      • SHOULD: Users should have control over page updates
        Web

        Do not change context on change of a <select> or radio button. Provide additional controls to allow the user to initiate that choice.

      • SHOULD: A method to turn off automatic content updates should be provided
        Web
      • SHOULD: A change of context should not be initated on focus or onChange event
        Web

        If modifying adjacent <select> controls on change of another <select> (for example for country, then state) these changes must be to elements that exist after the trigger element.

    • MUST: Users must be informed when a content change is pending
      Web

      Progress must be communicated to the user. If using a loading icon this should also be perceivable by assistive technologies. If using an aria live region, use the aria-busy attribute to stop any announcements to the user until the content change is complete.

    • MUST: Users must be informed when a content change has occurred
      Web

Technical

This section is composed entirely of concerns that are only those of the developers building and maintaining projects, and others with technical understanding such as QA.

  • HTML

    Summary

    http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Html

    HTML is the content layer of a website. All vital content must live in this layer, and not presentation information (which should live in the CSS).

    Rules

    • MUST: Markup must be semantically correct
      Web

      The code role="presentation" removes native semantics of an element, but the contents remain visible. When retrofitting content that inappropriately uses semantic elements like headings, tables, or lists use the presentation role to reset those semantics if appropriate.

      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: <em> or <strong> must be used to provide emphasis
        Web
      • MUST: <b>, <font> and <size> must not be used
        Web

        These elements are sometimes used to indicate headings. Any visual changes must live in CSS, not in markup.

      • MUST: <a> must be used when linking to a new resource, or to a named anchor within the page
        Web
      • MUST: Buttons must perform an action or submit a form
        Web

        They must not be used as a direct replacement for a standard link.

      • MUST: <blockquote> must only be used for quotations
        Web
      • MUST: Changes of state must be updated visually and programatically
        Web
      • MUST: Semantic markup must not be used for layout and styling
        Web

        For example, do not use <strong> to make something bold, if it should not be strongly emphasized from a semantics perspective. Instead use a <span> and font-weight:bold;.

    • MUST: Markup must be valid
      Web
      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: Compulsory attributes must be defined
        Web
      • MUST: Elements must not have duplicate attributes defined
        Web
      • MUST: Documents must not contain duplicate ID attribute values
        Web
      • MUST: Elements must be nested correctly
        Web
      • SHOULD: Elements should have complete start and end tags, or be self-closing if applicable
        Web

        It is recommended to use XHTML style syntax.

      • MUST: Direct descendents of parent elements must be valid
        Web For example, <li> is the only valid child of a <ul>.
      • MUST: Source markup must validate using
        Web
      • MUST: Dynamically modified markup must validate using
        Web
    • MUST: Deprecated tags must not be used
      Web

      Do not use deprecated tags (also known as obsolete). These include (but are not limited to) frame, frameset, acronym, font, big, center, strike, marquee, s, u, applet, basefont, bgsound, blink, dir, fn, isindex, layer, nobr, and tt.

    • MUST: The title attribute must be used with caution
      Web

      Information placed in the title attribute is not perceivable to all users or assistive technologies. This attribute cannot be used as a reliable method to create tooltip behavior, for example. Always use more robust labelling techniques.

    • MUST (WITH EXCEPTIONS): The page must not be automatically refreshed without the user's prior consent
      Web

      Do not use <meta http-equiv="refresh" /> as it will create an unexpected change of context that may interrupt the user. Server side redirects are okay. Where polling is absolutely necessary for a feature, this method is acceptable.

    • SHOULD: Sectioning elements should not be used
      Web

      Sectioning elements, although supported by modern browsers (in that they can be styled) cause confusing changes in context to assistive technologies. HTML 5 sectioning elements should be avoided (including <section> and <article>.

  • Structure

    Summary

    The structure of markup is important to convey the semantics and meaning of the content. With this information assistive technologies and other tools can expose the content in a more useful manner.

    Rules

    • MUST: The heading structure must be logical and comprehensible
      Web
      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: <h1> must identify the main content of the page
        Web

        <h1> should label the main content of the page, unless it is the homepage of a site, in which case the <h1> should label the site itself.

      • SHOULD: The first heading used on a page should be an <h1>
        Web

        This is the start of the document outline.

      • MUST: There must be at least one heading per page
        Web
      • MUST: Exactly one <h1> must be used per page
        Web

        You must not use multiple <h1> level headings on the same page.

      • MUST: Headings must be used in a logical, sequential order
        Web

        Do not skip levels. For example, <h2> can follow <h1>, but <h3> cannot.

      • MUST: Headings must be used to indicate sections of content
        Web

        Headings should be used to mark up section headings within main the content, demarcate different navigational sections like top nav or footer nav and mark up images of text used as headings. Only mark up content as headings if it has accompanying and sufficient content immediately following it.

      • MUST: Headings must be used to indicate the start of the main content
        Web
      • MUST: Headings must not be empty
        Web
      • MUST: <hgroup> must not be used
        Web

        This has been removed from the HTML spec, and is no longer valid.

      • MUST: Headings must not be used for styling
        Web

        Do not use headings to match a needed style from the CSS. Use the correct heading level for your content.

    • MUST: Lists must be marked up appropriately
      Web
      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: <ol> must be used to convey an ordered list of items
        Web

        Examples include points, steps, etc. Ordered lists must not be used for layout.

      • MUST: <ul> must be used when the order of the list is not important
        Web
      • MUST: Lists must not be empty
        Web
      • MUST: Lists must not be used for layout
        Web
    • MUST: Tables must be marked up appropriately
      Web

      Be sure to test tables in screen readers. Cell contents should be concise text followed by further elaboration if needed.

      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: <table>s must be used to markup tabular data and information
        Web
      • MUST: <table>s must not be used for layout and presentation
        Web
      • MUST: <th> must be used to markup row and column headings
        Web
      • MUST: <th> must not contain heading elements
        Web

        This is invalid HTML 5.

      • MUST: <th> tags must not use the abbr attribute
        Web

        The abbr attribute has been dropped from the spec. Minimum table markup includes <table>, <tr>, <th> and <td>.

      • SHOULD: <thead> or <tfoot> should be used along with <tbody>
        Web

        You can have multiple <tbody> tags. If you use <thead> or <tfoot> you must also provide a <tbody>.

      • SHOULD: <caption> should be used to provide a title for a table
        Web

        Tables can also be preceded by headings to label them. If so, use aria-labelledby to associate the heading instead of a caption.

      • MUST: <caption> must be an immediate child element of <table>
        Web
      • SHOULD: The <table> summary attribute should be used to describe how a table is organized
        Web

        Use the summary attribute to describe how a table is organized, but not to describe the contents of the table. summary attributes are not visible on screen. This is important for complex tables.

      • SHOULD: Complex tables should not be used
        Web

        If a table is too complex for a user to reasonably understand, consider breaking this table up into multiple tables or other presentations of the same data.

      • MUST: Empty cells must not be used to achieve a layout
        Web
    • MUST: Content must be in a logical and meaningful order
      Web
      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: Reading order must be the same as the visual order
        Web
      • SHOULD: Dynamic content should be placed in the DOM immediately after the triggering element
        Web

        This is so that it becomes the next thing in the tab order, e.g. when showing/hiding menus and dialogs. If visually reordering content, don't leave it to CSS, change it in the DOM so that the reading order matches what the user is seeing.

    • MUST: Landmark roles must be used appropriately
      Web
      Implementation Guidelines
      • SHOULD: The main page content should be identified with landmark role="main"
        Web

        You should also consider using the HTML5 <main> element in conjunction with role="main".

      • MUST: Labels for landmarks repeated across the site must be consistent
        Web

        This includes navigation, search, contentinfo, and so on.

      • MUST: Repeated landmark roles within the same page must be uniquely identifiable
        Web

        If multiple roles with role="navigation" are defined on the same page, use a label to differentiate them. The labels must be unique on the page. The aria-label attribute must not be left empty. When using role="search" have an aria-label that described functionality. If multiple roles with role="search" with different behavior on the same page, they must have an aria-label. The aria-label attribute can be set to the same string if the functionality is exactly the same. Headings can be used to label landmark sections

    • MUST: Content must be hidden appropriately
      Web
      Implementation Guidelines
      • SHOULD: CSS display: none; should be used to hide content both visually and from AT
        Web

        Other methods may be ineffective.

      • SHOULD: The Clipping method should be used to hide content visually if it should still be perceivable to assistive technologies
        Web

        Jonathan Snook's Clipping method is recommended - but a CSS class should be implemented for this, so that it is easy to change the method at a later date.

      • SHOULD: aria-hidden="true" should be used to hide visually displayed content from assistive technologies where appropriate
        Web

        aria-hidden="false" will not consistently expose elements to screen readers if they are still hidden using hidden="hidden", display:none or visibility:hidden.

      • MUST (WITH EXCEPTIONS): hidden="hidden" must not be used to hide content
        Web

        Do not rely on this property. It is not well supported yet.

        If using hidden=”hidden” to hide content, include the following CSS rule as a safety measure for older browsers.

        [hidden] { display: none !important; }

  • CSS

    Summary

    http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Css

    CSS is a presentation layer that sits on top of the content, and allows it to be styled appropriately. Many users choose to implement their own style sheets, or use other mechanisms that apply CSS to the Internet on top of the authors’ own. They may do this to implement color schemes that they find easier to read, or to increase the minimum font size. For this reason, the implemented CSS must not be too specific, since this makes it difficult for the user to override, and all of the presentation must be in CSS, and not in the markup.

    Rules

    • MUST: Content must be logical and comprehensible with CSS disabled
      Web
    • MUST: CSS must not be used to insert non-decorative content
      Web
    • SHOULD: Whitespace should be controlled with CSS
      Web
    • SHOULD: !important should not be used
      Web
    • SHOULD: Print stylesheets should be considered
      Web
    • SHOULD: iOS dynamic system font styles and sizes should be used
      Web

  • JavaScript

    Summary

    http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Javascript

    JavaScript is the behavior layer of a website, where application logic and behavior belong. It is important that the site works with JavaScript disabled, and that vital functionality is preserved. Progressive Enhancement must be preferred over Graceful Degradation.

    Rules

    • SHOULD: If achievable all functionality should work with JavaScript disabled
      Web
      Implementation Guidelines
      • SHOULD: Links should work when JavaScript is unavailable
        Web

        Links must have valid href attribute values to link to content, so that they still work with JavaScript disabled.

      • SHOULD: Users should be able to submit forms when JavaScript is disabled
        Web
      • SHOULD: JavaScript inline event handlers should not be used
        Web

        Do not use inline JavaScript event handlers e.g. on click or on submit. Aside from this being poor practise from a Separation of Concerns perspective, assistive technologies will have unpredictable behavior and it will show up as warnings in automated tools.

      • MUST: Interactive elements must be keyboard focusable and operable
        Web

        Don’t rely on JavaScript to make non-focusable elements (e.g. <div> or <span>) perform an action with a mouse. This is not good for keyboard-only users.

  • WAI-ARIA

    Summary

    http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WAI-ARIA

    WAI-ARIA defines methods for adding more semantics to markup, specifically with the intent of improving accessibility. As a general rule, WAI-ARIA should only be used as a last resort, when it is impossible to use standard markup to convey the appropriate semantics.

    Rules

    • MUST: WAI-ARIA must not be used unless necessary
      Web

      It is preferable to use native controls.

    • MUST: WAI-ARIA must be used correctly
      Web

      Use WAI-ARIA to expose content and interactions accessibly to users and assistive technology that cannot be made accessible using native HTML, or where a rebuild is not possible. Some attribute values are user-presented strings e.g. aria-label="user tools" or aria-valuetext="strongly agree". Attribute names are not user-presented strings e.g. for <input type="checkbox" checked="checked" /> VoiceOver will speak "checked checkbox". Roles define tokens, they are not user-presented strings e.g. for <input type="radio" /> VoiceOver will speak "radio button". Using labels with aria-label` or aria-labelledby will override native labelling mechanisms such as alt attributes, <label>s, or titles.

      Implementation Guidelines
      • MUST: Custom widget properties and states must be defined
        Web

        For example, aria-selected, aria-invalid or aria-expanded.

      • MUST: Custom widgets must be exposed using appropriate role
        Web

        For example, role="checkbox".

      • MUST: Custom widgets must have programmatically associated labels where appropriate
        Web

        For example, if creating a checkbox with role="checkbox", apply an aria-label or aria-labelledby.

      • MUST: Live regions must be created at page initialization
        Web

        You cannot insert live regions into the page, they must already exist on page load or they will not work. Don’t hide live regions via display:none (or similar) or they will stop being live regions.

      • SHOULD: Notifications should not move focus
        Web

        Alert users to page updates without moving focus by using WAI-ARIA live regions– e.g. <span aria-live="polite">1 found</span> or <span role="status">1 found</span>.

      • MUST: Overlays and dialogs must have a programatically associated heading
        Web

        It is necessary to use an aria-label if there is no appropriate heading in the content to label the modal dialog or overlay.

      • MUST: Roaming tabindex or the `aria-activedescendent` technique must be used to manage focus for custom container widgets
        Web

        A selected menu item should receive focus when menu is shown. The trigger button should receive focus when menu is hidden.

        You should never use both techniques simultaneously.

        Read more about the roaming tabindex technique

      • MUST: Assertive live regions must not be used
        Web

Welcome to AA+!

Introduction

This is an interactive website to reference Accessibility Plus (AA+) standard. It's designed to help you filter content that is directly relevant to you.

The table of contents can be used to jump straight to any section of your interest.

Filters

The filtering system will allow you to choose to only show particular types of items, or to filter by any of the following:

  • Roles
  • Impairments
  • Tags
  • WCAG rules

For example, implementation guidelines are typically more technical in nature, so if you are not an engineer you may find it useful to press "Filters" to open the filters overlay, choose to show only categories, subcategories and rules, then press "Apply Filter" to dismiss the overlay.

Filters will persist between sessions. Don't forget that in the filters overlay you can also press "Reset filters" to turn filtering off.

Versions

AA+ will automatically switch you to the latest version when one is published. You can switch to a previous version by going to the "versions" page via the footer links. You can also choose to diff any version with the version previous to it to see what's changed.

If you close AA+, the next time you open it it will switch to the latest version, so you'll always be up-to-date.

Glossary

A glossary is available via the "glossary" link in the footer

WCAG

AA+ is loosely based on the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines 2.0. For your convenience, we include a list of these via the WCAG link in the footer. These can be ordered by ID or by conformance level.
